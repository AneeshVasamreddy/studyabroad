Looking Back

Reflections

The semester abroad was never going to be neat. That became clear fast. Somewhere between a nine-hour bus ride back from the Sahara, a failed Environmental Economics exam discovered while already sick with food poisoning in Santorini, and the weekly mental arithmetic of whether you had enough left for the next trip, the cracks showed up. And somehow, that was still all part of it.

The money anxiety was constant. Four-euro beers in Prague felt almost comically cheap. Ten-euro pints in Dublin felt like a quiet insult. Amsterdam eight, Santorini three. Every city had its own math, and every week involved some version of the same calculation: budget, balance, push. There was never really a stretch where it was fully out of your head. You learned to live with that weight sitting somewhere in the background, always present, rarely stopping anything.

What kept making it worth it was the randomness of the people. A newly engaged couple from Belgium on the Morocco group bus, total strangers, who turned a twelve-hour desert transit into one of the more memorable conversations of the trip. University of Michigan guys in Barcelona, also studying abroad, who appeared one night and folded into everything like they had always been there. Middle-aged guys from the Netherlands sharing a sauna on Canary Wharf in London, completely unbothered, talking about cities and life like it was the most natural setting for it. Eighteen-year-olds from the Netherlands in Lisbon, barely legal, already traveling alone. The world kept showing up in places you did not plan for it, and those were usually the best moments.

The worst day was Day 2 in Athens. Food poisoning, stuck in the room, unable to move, and then the notification: failed the Environmental Economics exam, resit required. Friends were somewhere out in the city having a good time. There is not a clean lesson in that stretch of hours. It just happened. Then Day 3 came, and I got up, went to Mitropoleos Square, kept moving. That is not wisdom. That is just what you do when staying still feels worse than anything else. Easy to say keep pushing when you are already in Greece, obviously. But the hard parts were real, and the good parts were better than almost anything I have done.

Sixteen cities. Eleven countries. The Sahara one week, the Acropolis the next, Szechenyi Baths the week after. The blue domes of Santorini. The Berlin Wall, which does not let you be casual about it. Ibiza on a Sunday night with ANOTR at Pacha, which was probably the best single night of the semester. The grand scale of it only settles now that it is over. You were moving too fast to notice it while it was happening. That is what I carry: not just the highlights, but the full picture. The nights that did not go right and the mornings that made up for them. The reel, all of it, every part.
